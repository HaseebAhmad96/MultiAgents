Artificial intelligence is rapidly transforming the global economy, with businesses across 
all sectors rushing to integrate machine learning models into their core operations. 
Companies like Google, Microsoft, and OpenAI are investing billions into AI research, 
fuelling an arms race that shows no signs of slowing down.

However, this progress comes with growing concerns. Millions of workers fear that 
automation will displace their jobs, particularly in fields like customer service, data entry, 
and transportation. Governments are struggling to keep up with the pace of change, and 
regulatory frameworks remain fragmented and often inadequate.

At the same time, AI is delivering remarkable benefits in healthcare. Diagnostic tools 
powered by deep learning can now detect cancers and rare diseases with accuracy rivalling 
expert clinicians. Drug discovery timelines that once took decades are being compressed 
into years. Patients in underserved regions are gaining access to specialist-level 
consultations through AI-powered telemedicine platforms.

The environmental footprint of AI is another pressing issue. Training large language models 
consumes enormous amounts of electricity, and data centres are placing increasing strain 
on power grids. Researchers are actively working on more efficient architectures, but the 
energy demand continues to outpace efficiency gains for now.

Despite these challenges, optimism remains high among technologists. They argue that 
every major technological revolution in history — from the printing press to the internet — 
created far more jobs than it destroyed, and that AI will be no different. The key, they say, 
lies in proactive investment in education and reskilling programmes to help workers adapt 
to the new landscape.
